The Question Mark That Broke the VPN

Published: Jan 20, 2026 • 4 min read • Network Security

Sometimes the smallest character can cause the biggest headaches. Here's how a single question mark in a password brought down an entire site-to-site VPN tunnel — and what I learned from it.

The Call

It was a routine Tuesday when I got the call. A customer's site-to-site VPN tunnel was down, and they couldn't connect to their remote office. These tunnels are critical — when they're down, entire teams can't access shared resources, servers, or applications. The pressure was on.

Initial Troubleshooting

I jumped on a Zoom call with the customer to troubleshoot together. First step: the basics. I tried bouncing the tunnel (bringing it down and back up). No luck. The tunnel stayed stubbornly offline.

Next, I dove into the firewall logs. That's where things got interesting. The logs showed a clear error: authentication mismatch. This usually means the pre-shared key (PSK) — the password both sides of the VPN use to authenticate — doesn't match.

The Plot Thickens

I asked the customer to verify the PSK configured on their end. They read it out. I checked our side. They matched perfectly. Character by character. So why was authentication failing?

Then the customer mentioned something crucial: "Oh, by the way, this issue started after we changed the peer IP address. We also updated the PSK at the same time."

Bingo. Something about that PSK change triggered this. But what?

Down the Rabbit Hole

I started searching Cisco documentation, forums, and knowledge base articles. After digging through several threads about PSK authentication issues, I stumbled upon a buried note in the Cisco ASA documentation that made everything click:

Warning: Pre-shared keys on Cisco ASA should not contain the question mark (?) character. The ASA parses the question mark as a string separator, which can cause the PSK to be truncated during authentication.

I looked at the PSK again. Sure enough: SecureP@ss?word123

That innocent question mark was silently breaking everything. The ASA was only using SecureP@ss for authentication, while the other end was using the full string. No wonder authentication failed.

The Fix

The solution was straightforward once we understood the problem:

  1. Generated a new pre-shared key without any special characters that could cause parsing issues
  2. Updated the PSK on our ASA firewall
  3. Had the customer update their end with the same new PSK
  4. Bounced the tunnel one more time

The tunnel came up immediately. Green lights across the board. The customer confirmed they could access all their remote resources.

Lessons Learned

This experience taught me several valuable lessons:

1. Context is Everything

When the customer mentioned they'd recently changed both the peer IP and PSK, that was the key piece of information. Always ask "What changed?" when troubleshooting.

2. Special Characters Aren't Always Special

While complex passwords with special characters are important for security, not all systems handle them the same way. Certain characters have special meaning in specific contexts (like ? being a separator in ASA).

3. Document Vendor Quirks

This obscure limitation isn't immediately obvious. Now I keep a running list of platform-specific gotchas like this for quick reference during troubleshooting.

4. Persistence Pays Off

When the obvious solutions don't work, dig deeper. The answer was buried in documentation, but it was there. Persistence and thorough research make the difference between a quick fix and hours of frustration.

The Takeaway

Sometimes troubleshooting feels like detective work. You gather clues, form hypotheses, and test them until something clicks. This case reminded me that even the smallest details — a single character — can have outsized impacts on enterprise infrastructure.

And now, whenever I configure VPN tunnels, I make sure to avoid question marks in PSKs. Lesson learned.

Pro tip: If you're configuring Cisco ASA pre-shared keys, stick to alphanumeric characters and common special characters like @, #, !, and %. Avoid ?, /, \, and " to prevent parsing issues.
