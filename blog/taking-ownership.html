Taking Ownership: When a Two-Hour Fix Feels Like a Win

Jan 22, 2026•6 min read•Leadership

Sometimes the hardest part of solving a problem isn't the technical work — it's navigating the human elements. This is a story about inheriting a frustrated customer, coordinating multiple teams, and learning when to ask for help.

Walking Into a Storm

The ticket landed in my queue with a note that made my stomach sink: "Customer is extremely frustrated. Requesting escalation."

I pulled up the ticket history. A customer's server had been compromised and needed to be isolated in a separate network segment immediately. The request had come in days ago. The previous engineer had taken the call but never updated the customer or worked on the ticket. Radio silence.

Now the customer was understandably angry. When security is compromised, every hour counts. And this ticket had sat untouched for days.

Step 1: Own It

I called the customer immediately. His frustration was palpable from the first sentence.

I didn't make excuses. I didn't blame the previous engineer. I apologized for the delay and told him explicitly: "I'm taking full ownership of this ticket. This will get resolved today, and I'll keep you updated every step of the way."

I asked him to give me some time to review the full ticket and prepare a solution. He agreed — cautiously. I'd bought myself maybe an hour of goodwill. I needed to make it count.

Step 2: Understand the Scope

I read through the entire ticket from start to finish. The requirement was clear but complex:

  1. Create a new network segment with a fresh IP block and VLAN
  2. Configure VLANs across switches, aggregators, firewalls, and hypervisors
  3. Trunk the VLAN to appropriate switch ports
  4. Configure the new firewall segment with strict isolation rules
  5. Block all traffic to other network segments except RDP from their office IP
  6. Migrate the compromised server to the new segment with new IP addressing

This touched six different infrastructure components: firewalls, switches, aggregators, routers, hypervisors, and the virtualization platform. I knew firewalls inside and out, but switches and routers? That wasn't my strong suit.

Step 3: Know When to Ask for Help

Here's where ego could have gotten in the way. I could have tried to muddle through the switch configurations, spending hours Googling syntax and hoping I got it right. On a normal ticket, maybe that's fine.

But this wasn't a normal ticket. This customer had already waited too long. I didn't have time to learn switching on the fly. More importantly, this was a security-critical change. One misconfiguration and we could expose the compromised server to the very networks we were trying to isolate it from.

I needed backup.

I reached out to a senior engineer on my team who had deep switching and routing experience. I explained the situation: frustrated customer, time-sensitive security issue, and I needed his expertise to validate my change plan and handle the components I wasn't confident in.

He agreed to join the call.

Step 4: Coordinate the Chaos

I got back on the Zoom call with the customer, this time with my senior colleague. I'd prepared a detailed change plan for the firewall segments and access rules. He reviewed the switching and routing components.

Then we realized we needed more people. Since the server was virtualized, we needed:

  • Server team: To reassign IP addressing at the OS level
  • Virtualization team: To move the VM to the correct VLAN and network segment

I got both teams on the call. Now we had six people in the Zoom: me, my senior colleague, the customer, and three specialists from different infrastructure teams.

It could have been chaos. Instead, we worked methodically:

Phase 1: Network Foundation

My senior colleague configured the VLANs across switches and aggregators. I handled the firewall segment creation and isolation rules.

Phase 2: Virtualization

The virtualization team moved the VM to the new VLAN and verified connectivity at the hypervisor level.

Phase 3: Server Configuration

The server team logged into the VM and reconfigured the network interface with the new private IP addressing.

Phase 4: Access Rules

I worked with the customer to identify which office IPs needed RDP access and configured the firewall rules accordingly.

Step 5: Verification

After all changes were complete, we tested systematically:

  • ✓ Server responding on new IP address
  • ✓ RDP accessible from customer's office
  • ✓ Traffic blocked to all other internal segments
  • ✓ External access blocked except approved IPs

Everything worked. The compromised server was fully isolated but still accessible to authorized users.

The Aftermath

The customer tested access from his end. He got in immediately. Then he said something that stuck with me:

"This was a two-hour call. We got six people together, made the changes, tested everything, and it's done. The last engineer had this ticket for days and didn't even respond. Thank you for actually taking ownership."

He wasn't just thanking me for the technical fix. He was thanking me for the follow-through, the communication, and the coordination.

What I Learned

1. Ownership isn't just about doing the work yourself

Taking ownership means ensuring the problem gets solved, even if that means bringing in other people with better expertise than you. I owned the outcome, not every keystroke.

2. Communication beats speed

The customer wasn't mad about the complexity or the time it took. He was mad about the silence. Keeping him updated and involved turned his frustration into appreciation.

3. Know your limits, but don't let them limit you

I wasn't an expert in switching and virtualization. But I knew who was, and I wasn't too proud to ask for help. That's not weakness — that's resourcefulness.

4. Security incidents require urgency

When a server is compromised, every hour matters. Understanding the stakes changes how you prioritize and coordinate. This wasn't just a network change — it was an active security response.

The Bigger Picture

This ticket taught me that some of the most valuable work in IT isn't purely technical. It's about:

  • Stepping up when things fall through the cracks
  • Building trust with frustrated customers through transparency
  • Coordinating specialists across teams to solve complex problems
  • Knowing when to lead and when to defer to expertise

Would the problem have gotten solved eventually without me? Probably. But the customer needed someone to own it right now, communicate clearly, and drive it to completion.

Sometimes that's the most important role you can play.

Takeaway: True ownership isn't about doing everything yourself. It's about making sure the right things happen, even when that means orchestrating other people's expertise.
