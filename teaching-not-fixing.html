When Going the Extra Mile Means Teaching, Not Just Fixing

Jan 18, 2026•4 min read•Customer Support

Sometimes "going above and beyond" isn't about doing more work — it's about taking the time to help someone truly understand what's happening. This is the story of a VPN troubleshooting call that turned into a teaching moment.

The Problem

A customer called in frustrated. He couldn't connect to Cisco AnyConnect VPN, and even when he did manage to connect, he still couldn't access his server. He'd been trying for hours and was understandably stressed.

"I can't connect to the VPN, and even when I do, the server won't respond. Nothing is working."

Step 1: See What They See

Rather than troubleshooting blind, I immediately jumped on a Zoom session with him. This is something I always try to do when possible — visibility matters. I needed to see exactly what he was experiencing: what IP he was trying to connect to, what error messages he was getting, and how he was attempting the connection.

Watching someone navigate through an issue in real-time gives you context that's impossible to get over email or phone alone.

Step 2: The First Issue - VPN Authentication

First things first: the VPN connection itself. He was entering his credentials but getting authentication failures. This was straightforward — I reset his password on the firewall and provided him with the new credentials.

He tried again. Success! The AnyConnect client showed "Connected" with a green checkmark. One problem down.

Step 3: The Real Issue - Understanding VPNs

Now connected to the VPN, he tried to RDP (Remote Desktop) to his server again. Still failing.

I asked him to share his screen and show me exactly what he was typing. That's when I saw it: he was trying to connect using the server's public IP address.

And that's when I realized — this wasn't just a technical issue. It was a conceptual one. He didn't understand how VPNs work.

The Teaching Moment

I could have just said "use the private IP instead" and ended the call. Problem solved, ticket closed.

But I took a different approach. I explained:

"Think of a VPN like a tunnel into your office network. Before you connected to the VPN, you were outside the network — like standing on the street. The only way to reach your office computers from the street is through their public-facing addresses, which might be blocked by security."

"But now that you're connected to AnyConnect, you're inside the network. You've walked through the tunnel and you're in the building. Now you don't need to use public addresses anymore — you can access everything using internal, private addresses. That's the whole point of the VPN."

"So instead of connecting to the public IP (which the firewall now sees as coming from inside and blocks), you need to use the server's private IP. That's how everything on the internal network talks to each other."

The Lightbulb Moment

I watched his cursor move to the RDP connection dialog. He deleted the public IP and typed in the private IP I provided.

Connected.

His server desktop appeared on screen. He was in. But more importantly, he understood why it worked now.

"Oh! So once I'm on the VPN, I'm basically on the same network as the server. That makes so much sense now. I was treating it like I was still connecting from outside."

Exactly.

Why This Matters

This call took maybe 20 minutes total. I could have resolved it in 5 by just giving him the private IP and moving on. But that extra 15 minutes spent explaining why made all the difference.

Here's what we actually accomplished:

  • Fixed his immediate VPN authentication issue
  • Resolved his server access problem
  • Taught him how VPNs actually work
  • Prevented future tickets where he'd make the same mistake
  • Built his confidence in troubleshooting network issues

The best customer service isn't just solving problems — it's empowering people to understand and solve problems themselves in the future.

Lessons I've Learned

1. Visual troubleshooting is worth it

Getting on a screen share might feel like it takes longer, but it's almost always faster than back-and-forth over chat or phone. You catch things you'd never notice otherwise.

2. Assume good intent, not ignorance

This customer wasn't being difficult or dense. He just didn't have the context to understand how VPNs work at a networking level. Once he had that context, everything clicked instantly.

3. Teach, don't just fix

Taking the time to explain why something works builds trust and reduces future support load. That customer now knows more about VPNs than he did an hour ago. That's a win.

The Follow-Up

A few weeks later, I got another ticket from the same customer. But this time, it was different:

"Hey Aditya, I'm trying to access a different server and running into issues. I made sure I'm using the private IP this time since I'm connected via VPN, but it's still timing out. Can you help me check if there's a firewall rule blocking me?"

He'd learned. He knew what to check first, and he knew what information I'd need to help troubleshoot further. That's what good support looks like.

Takeaway: Going above and beyond doesn't always mean working harder. Sometimes it means slowing down, explaining clearly, and giving someone the knowledge they need to succeed on their own.
