Email Authentication Protocols: SPF, DKIM, and DMARC Explained

Jan 10, 2026•7 min read

Email authentication protocols are your first line of defense against phishing, spoofing, and impersonation attacks. Here's how SPF, DKIM, and DMARC work together to protect your domain and improve deliverability.

The Email Security Problem

Email was designed in an era when security wasn't a priority. By default, anyone can claim to send email from any domain. This makes phishing and spoofing trivially easy for attackers.

Authentication protocols solve this by giving domain owners ways to:

  • Specify which servers can send email on their behalf (SPF)
  • Cryptographically sign their messages (DKIM)
  • Tell receivers what to do with unauthenticated emails (DMARC)

SPF (Sender Policy Framework)

SPF lets you publish a list of authorized mail servers for your domain via DNS records.

How it works: When a mail server receives email claiming to be from your domain, it checks your SPF record to verify the sending server is authorized.

v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com include:amazonses.com ~all

This record says: "Accept email from Google's servers and Amazon SES. Soft-fail everything else."

DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)

DKIM adds a cryptographic signature to your emails, proving they weren't tampered with in transit and confirming the sender's identity.

How it works: Your mail server signs outgoing messages with a private key. Receivers verify the signature using your public key (published in DNS).

Unlike SPF which checks the sending server, DKIM validates the message itself. This means forwarded emails can still pass DKIM checks.

DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication)

DMARC ties SPF and DKIM together and tells receivers what to do when authentication fails.

DMARC policies:

  • p=none — Monitor only, don't take action
  • p=quarantine — Send suspicious emails to spam
  • p=reject — Block unauthenticated emails entirely

DMARC also provides reporting, so you can see who's trying to send email claiming to be from your domain.

Implementation Strategy

  1. Start with SPF: Identify all legitimate sending sources and create your SPF record
  2. Add DKIM: Configure your mail servers or ESP to sign messages
  3. Deploy DMARC in monitor mode: Use p=none to collect data without impacting delivery
  4. Analyze reports: Identify any legitimate sources you missed
  5. Gradually enforce: Move to p=quarantine, then p=reject as confidence builds

Pro tip: Monitor your DMARC reports regularly. They reveal phishing attempts against your domain and help identify misconfigured services trying to send on your behalf.
